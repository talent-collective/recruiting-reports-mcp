# iCIMS — May 2026 Workforce Report: AI Reshaping Early Career Hiring

**Source:** https://www.icims.com/company/newsroom/mayinsights2026/
**Published:** 2026-05-21
**Data:** Proprietary iCIMS platform data from 3M+ global platform users and 691M+ candidate profiles; supplemented by survey of entry-level job seekers (ages 18–24)
**Period:** Platform data reflects April 2026 vs. March 2025 baseline; survey conducted spring 2026
**Best for:** Entry-level hiring gap analysis, AI's impact on early career expectations, April 2026 labor market snapshot, candidate confidence benchmarks

---

## April 2026 Labor Market Snapshot

- **Job openings** hit a 12-month peak: **+15%** above March 2025 baseline
- **Application volume dropped 10%** despite record openings
- **Hiring velocity flatlined at 0%** growth — openings and applicants growing in opposite directions

## Entry-Level Hiring Disconnect

| Metric | April 2026 Change |
|---|---|
| Entry-level job openings | +18% (led by manufacturing and retail) |
| Entry-level applications | -9% |
| Entry-level hires | +3% |

- The entry-level hiring gap is widening: openings surging while applicant supply contracts
- Manufacturing and retail are the primary drivers of the opening surge

## Entry-Level Job Seeker Attitudes Toward AI

- **78%** of entry-level job seekers (ages 18–24) believe AI and automation are changing the volume *and* nature of entry-level roles
- **54%** believe employers now expect entry-level candidates to already have mid-level experience
- **37%** are hesitant to trust AI in the hiring process — most citing its impersonal nature as the primary concern

## Candidate Confidence and Job Security

- Only **19%** of entry-level job seekers say they feel "very confident" in their careers
- **29%** report low or no confidence in their career trajectory
- **44%** of entry-level job seekers cite job security as their top factor when evaluating roles — up from prior years

## Key Conclusions

- The entry-level hiring market faces a structural paradox: openings are at a 12-month peak while applications and hires have stalled, signaling a supply-side breakdown among early career talent
- AI anxiety is tangible and measurable: nearly 4 in 5 Gen Z job seekers believe AI is reshaping the entry-level market, yet most are not confident in how to respond
- Employers inflating experience requirements for entry-level roles are accelerating the supply contraction — 54% of candidates perceive mid-level experience is now expected
- Job security has emerged as the dominant evaluation criterion for entry-level candidates, surpassing compensation in salience — a signal that stability messaging matters more than ever
- Organizations that address AI transparency and communicate stability in their EVP have a clear opening to differentiate on candidate experience
