# Ashby: The State of Startup Hiring | 2026 Talent Trends Report

## Key Takeaways

- AI adoption is no longer fringe — 60% of startup talent teams using AI across hiring workflows as of Q3 2025
- Remote roles drive 42% more inbound applications AND 9% higher offer acceptance rates
- Startups that involve recruiters earlier hire ~30% faster at the smallest stages
- Ashby supports 52% of the fastest-growing startups on Brex's 2025 Benchmarks list
- Most startup hires come from inbound, followed by sourcing; referrals make up 15% (slightly less than all-company 18%)
- Offer acceptance rates hover around 80% — consistent with the all-company benchmark

---

## I. Introduction

As a YC startup itself, Ashby views the startup community as one of its most meaningful segments. Startups move first — with fewer constraints than enterprise orgs, startup talent leaders are frequently the earliest adopters of new tooling, experimenting with AI long before it becomes standard practice.

This report covers:
1. How AI is taking shape inside startup organizations and workflows
2. The hiring lifecycle end-to-end — inbound, sourcing, interviews, offer acceptance
3. Clear benchmarks across the recruiting funnel, segmented by startup size

Most data is from 2025, with some trend charts extending further back.

---

## II. The Rise of AI

### AI Startups

- ".ai" domains increased from **5% of startups in 2023 → 16% by end of 2025** (11% increase)
- 64% of all U.S. venture capital dollars invested in AI startups in 2025 (Pitchbook/NVCA)

### AI in Job Descriptions

Looking across 11M startup job applications in Ashby:
- **Percent of jobs with "AI" in the title doubled** (2% → 4%)
- Titles with "data" remained flat at 3% across the same period
- **~33% of all job postings mention AI** — tech roles mention it more, but both tech and business show significant increases

### AI in Recruiting Workflows

- Steady increase every quarter over the past 2 years
- **60% of startup customers using AI in Q3 2025** (up from a much smaller base)
- **10% use 5+ features actively** — fully adopting AI across the hiring lifecycle
- Most users concentrate on 1–2 features regularly
- Larger startups use more AI — growing pains (increased hiring volume, interviewer load, coordination complexity) create stronger incentives to adopt broadly
- These usage metrics are a **lower bound** — don't include AI usage via Ashby integration partners

AI is distributed across the hiring lifecycle, not concentrated in a single area:
- Sourcing: AI-generated email tokens for personalized outreach (e.g., French startup Amo)
- Feedback: AI-generated feedback summaries to keep stakeholders aligned (e.g., one-woman talent team at Amsterdam-based Altura)
- Notetaking: native AI Notetaker (released 2025) helping teams maintain hiring momentum
- Application review: AI-assisted review for parsing high-volume inbound

## III. Inbound & Sourcing

### Source of Hire (startup breakdown)

Hires by source, across startup sizes:
- **Inbound**: most hires across all sizes
- **Sourced**: second most common; most dramatic for smallest startups (30% of hires come from sourcing)
- **Referrals**: **15% of startup hires** (vs. 18% across all company sizes)
  - Smaller than expected given the emphasis early founding teams place on network hiring
  - Referrals grow as a share as startups scale
- As startups grow, the mix shifts — sourcing dominates early, inbound grows with scale

**Prior Ashby finding (all company sizes):** fewer hires were coming from referral sources over time while more come from inbound. Startup data mirrors this but with slightly lower referral share overall.

### Sourcing Benchmarks (startups vs. all companies)

Despite lower brand recognition than larger orgs:
- Startup email open rate: **~81%** (vs. all-company ~86%)
- Reply rate: **~19%** (matches all-company average)
- Overall conversion: on par with broader market

## IV. Application Volume

### Inbound Volume by Startup Size

- Smallest startups receive the fewest inbound applications (expected)
- All startup sizes are experiencing a similar increase in inbound volume as the broader market since 2021
- The surge in applications that began in 2024 affects startups proportionally

**Benefit for startups:** More talent availability can influence some candidates to consider roles outside their initial search parameters (e.g., early stage vs. growth stage, lesser-known brand). Challenge: managing the volume.

### Remote vs. In-Office Applications

- **Remote startup jobs receive 42% more inbound applications** than in-office startup jobs
- Pattern holds consistently across all startup sizes

### Remote Work Breakdown

- **44%** of venture-backed startups using Ashby have almost all jobs with remote options
- **35%** have no full remote option
- **21%** provide a mix (some remote, some not)
- Most startups (79%) go all-in on one or the other — very few offer a hybrid policy
- Share of startup jobs with remote options has slightly **declined**: ~80% in 2023 → ~60% in 2025 (slow shift toward in-person)

## V. The Time Cost of a Hire

### Interviews per Hire

- **For every hire made, 15 applicants receive an interview**
  - Technical hire: **18 applicants** receive an interview
  - Business hire: **13 applicants** receive an interview

Quote from Heather Doshay (Former Partner, Head of Talent, SignalFire):
> "This data confirms that more interviews don't lead to better outcomes. High-performing startups define competencies up front, and run only high-signal steps. That creates a better candidate experience and keeps interviewer time from becoming a hidden tax."

### Interviewer Hours per Hire

- Larger startups tend to have more interviewer hours per hire (~29 hours)
- Business roles: relatively flat across all startup sizes
- Technical roles: scales with startup size
  - <25 employees: **~21 interviewer hours** per technical hire
  - 100–300 employees: **~29 interviewer hours** per technical hire
- Note: figures represent time in scheduled interviews only; does not include take-home assignments or async work

### Candidate Interview Hours

- Hired candidate at a startup spends **2.5–3 hours interviewing**
- Benchmark comparison: all-company average (from 2025 Recruiter Productivity report) is 2.5–3.5 hours — startups are at the lower end of the range
- 25th–75th percentile range spans ~2 hours (meaningful variation)
- Technical candidates undergo a longer interview process than business candidates
- Figures exclude take-home assignments

## VI. Offer Acceptance Rates

Overall startup OAR: **~80%**

*Note on methodology: some smaller startups skip formal offer letters; this analysis looks specifically at offer stage rates in Ashby. Actual OAR may look different depending on how teams track offers.*

### Remote vs. In-Office OAR

Remote roles see **9% higher offer acceptance rates** over in-office:
- **Technical remote: +13%** higher acceptance rate
- **Business remote: +4%** higher acceptance rate
- Exception: startups with <25 employees see **no difference** in acceptance rates for technical roles (remote vs. in-office)

**Combined with application volume data:** remote roles attract 42% more applicants AND convert offers at a higher rate. Strong case for remote across most startup sizes.

### Trend: Remote Work Availability

Despite OAR and application volume advantages, the share of venture-backed startup jobs with remote options has slightly declined:
- ~80% of jobs had remote options in 2023
- ~60% in 2025
- Slow but steady shift toward in-person requirements

## VII. The Teams Behind Startup Hiring

### Recruiter Involvement

Smaller startups may have no designated TA team; larger startups are solidifying process and hiring dedicated recruiters. Analysis looks at percentage of jobs (with a hire) that have a recruiter or hiring manager assigned.

- Hiring managers play a key role across all startup sizes
- As startups grow, the delta between hiring manager involvement and recruiter involvement narrows
- Note: a job without a recruiter assigned doesn't mean there's no TA team — a team may have built the hiring architecture without being assigned to individual jobs

### Recruiter Involvement and Time to Hire

- **<25 employees: time to hire drops ~30% when a recruiter is involved**
- For larger startups: recruiter involvement still improves TTH, but the difference is less stark — existing process architecture provides benefits even when a recruiter isn't explicitly assigned
- Data supports investing in a talent professional earlier than most startups do

### Hiring Team Size (talent team members involved, excl. hiring managers)

| Startup Size | Avg. People Involved in Hiring |
|---|---|
| <25 employees | 4.5 |
| 25–49 employees | ~5.1 |
| 50–99 employees | ~6.1 |
| 100–300 employees | ~8.1 |

Larger startups often have double the number of talent team members involved compared to smaller startups.

## Closing Thoughts

Startup hiring in 2025 was defined by scale and speed — more applications, more tooling, more pressure to move efficiently without sacrificing quality.

AI is no longer theoretical for startup talent teams. It's embedded across sourcing, screening, and coordination, helping teams manage volume and reclaim time. Remote roles continue to reshape candidate behavior — driving both higher application volume and stronger offer acceptance.

The fundamentals remain consistent: startups that invest earlier in recruiting support move faster, reduce interviewer load, and create more momentum across the funnel. Even as company size, brand recognition, and hiring models vary widely, thoughtful process design paired with the right technology produces measurable advantages from day one.
