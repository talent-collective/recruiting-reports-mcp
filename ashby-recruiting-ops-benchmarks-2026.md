# Ashby: Recruiting Operations Benchmarks | 2026 Talent Trends Report

## Key Takeaways

- Hiring becomes more predictable as systems mature, with Time to First Fill stabilizing as organizations scale
- Time to Hire is shaped by many small delays across the process, not a single bottleneck
- Automated scheduling is 26% faster than manual (3.7 hrs vs. 5 hrs median)
- Referrals consistently outperform all other sources at nearly every funnel stage
- Process discipline directly impacts data quality and candidate experience, particularly in feedback and archiving

---

## Overview

Hiring is often measured through familiar metrics — Time to Fill, Time to Hire, passthrough rates. But these are outputs of a much larger system. This report takes a Recruiting Operations perspective, breaking down different parts of the system: how long it takes to fill a role, how candidates move through interviews, and how source strategy shapes outcomes.

---

## I. Time to First Fill

Time to First Fill = time from when a job was opened to when the first hire was made. Two primary factors shape it:

### Organizational Scale

- Smaller organizations show a wide distribution — startups can move fast but also drag on much longer
- As organizations grow, variability narrows and timelines standardize
- ~100 employees is where TTF begins to stabilize — earlier than when most teams make their first RecOps hire (typically 200–500 employees)
- The inflection point: when TTF becomes consistent enough to support accurate headcount planning and stakeholder alignment — but before process debt compounds

### Role Complexity

- Senior roles take **37% longer** to fill than junior positions
- Technical roles take ~15 more days to fill than business roles
- Technical roles consistently take longer across all prior reporting
- A single TTF target across all functions creates misleading signals — some teams appear slower when they're operating within expected norms
- Technical roles tend to receive more applications per hire, involve more interview stages, and require deeper evaluation — all of which extend timelines

---

## II. Where Time Is Spent in Hiring

Time to Hire = sum of delays across every step. It is NOT driven by a single bottleneck — companies with generally good TTH may still struggle with one or two specific bottlenecks.

Top and bottom quartile companies differ across four key process areas:

### 1. Application Review

**The bottleneck:** Review is often the first point of delay, especially for high-volume roles. Technical roles tend to receive more applications per hire and require more evaluation per candidate — hired candidates in tech spend more time in review than business. The gap in review time between high-performing and slower companies is greater for tech than business.

**What helps:** AI-assisted application review — defining clear criteria upfront and using AI to evaluate against those criteria. Reduces triage time, improves consistency across reviewers, and focuses human attention on high-signal candidates.

### 2. Scheduling

**The bottleneck:** Two distinct components:
- **Total Scheduling Duration** — time from entering a stage to when a schedule is sent
- **Schedule Lead Time** — time between schedule being sent and the interview taking place

Schedule lead time is the larger bottleneck. Even when interviews are scheduled quickly, limited interviewer availability pushes interviews days out. One of the most controllable levers for improving TTH.

**What helps:**
- Auto Schedule (Ashby): confirms interviews **26% faster** than manual (3.7 hrs vs. 5 hrs median). 1.3 hrs saved compounds across multiple stages.
- Expanding interviewer pools — more qualified interviewers = more scheduling flexibility = less lead time delay

### 3. Feedback and Decision-Making

**The bottleneck:** Time between the final interview and a hiring decision is one of the most significant sources of delay. Delays occur not because feedback is missing, but because it's incomplete, inconsistent, or hard to synthesize across interviewers. Improving feedback speed and quality are directly intertwined.

**What helps:** AI Notetaker — automatically captures interview notes, generates summaries, provides transcript access. Results from prior Ashby analysis:
- Scorecard submission within 5 hours: **~9% higher** when AI Notetaker is used
- Scorecard submission within 24 hours: **~6% higher** (70% vs. 66%)

### 4. Offer Approvals

**The bottleneck:** Time between candidate entering the offer stage and an offer being created. Involves multiple stakeholders (finance, people, leadership), approvals, and manual steps. Once a decision is made, these delays simply extend time-to-seat.

**What helps:** Offer templates with built-in controls — standardize offers across roles, reduce manual edits and back-and-forth, automate approvals where possible.

## III. Interviews

### 1. Scheduling Complexity

- Auto Schedule confirms interviews **26% faster** than manual
- Automated: median **3.7 hours**
- Manual: median **5 hours**
- 1.3 hours saved compounds across multi-stage interview loops

### 2. Interview Structure

- Hired technical candidates: ~**18 days** to complete full interview loop (screen → final)
- Hired business candidates: ~**14 days** from first to last interview
- A single interview lifecycle benchmark applied across both makes technical hiring look slower than it is

### 3. Take-Home Assignments

- ~**13% of hires** included a take-home component
- Usage is not uniform — it shifts by application volume:
  - Business roles: climbs from ~8% at lower volumes to 20%+ at highest application ranges (teams lean on async evaluation when capacity is constrained)
  - Technical roles: flat at 10–14% regardless of volume
- Suggests business hiring uses take-homes as a volume management tool more than technical hiring does

### 4. Passthrough Rates by Stage

- **Recruiter screen:** ~35% passthrough (handles the highest candidate volume)
- **Post-onsite:** ~95%
- **Offer:** ~81%
- Confidence in candidates builds as they move through the process; early stages filter broadly, later stages are high-conviction

### 5. Scorecard Alignment

- ~**38% of scorecard pairs** include at least one point difference between interviewers
- Nearly half of those one-point differences fall between scores of 2 and 3 — crossing the yes/no threshold on Ashby's 1–4 scale
- When disagreements cross that line, they tend to increase time in process and delay decisions
- Misalignment within score values typically sparks additional internal conversations, compounding delay

**Scorecard completion rates by org size:**
- <25 employees: ~**49%**
- 500+ employees: ~**72%**
- Completion is also higher for hired candidates than rejected ones — interviewers invest more in feedback when a candidate has a chance of moving forward
- Incomplete scorecards for rejected candidates create data gaps and limit the team's ability to learn from patterns across the full pipeline

## IV. Funnel Strategy: The Impact of Referrals

### Overall Referral Performance

- **52% of referred candidates pass initial screens** vs. 35% overall
- Higher passthrough rates at nearly every stage
- Higher offer acceptance rates than both inbound and sourced candidates — across both business and technical roles
- Gap is especially notable in technical hiring: sourced tech candidates accept at lower rates than referred candidates

### Not All Referrals Are Equal

Referrer job function plays a meaningful role in outcomes:
- Referrals more likely to result in interviews when **referrer works in the same function** as the role being filled (tech referring tech, business referring business)
- This becomes even more pronounced at the hire stage
- Out-of-function referrals are often given an initial opportunity through interviews, but their conversion to hires is lower
- Within-function referrals carry more signal; out-of-function referrals are not without value, but comparatively weaker

**Consistent with prior Ashby reporting:** referrals show strong conversion from application to interview and interview to offer, second only to internal candidates.

## V. Archiving

Archiving = closing out every candidate who did not get hired. Time to Archive is a meaningful KPI for data quality, process discipline, and candidate experience.

### Archiving Speed and Candidate NPS

NPS scores hold relatively steady through the first 9 days, then fall sharply once candidates cross the 3-week mark:

| Time to Archive | Median NPS |
|-----------------|------------|
| Within 1 day | **9** |
| Days 10–24 | **7** |
| 50+ days | **3** |

(NPS scale: 0–6 = detractors, 7–8 = passives, 9–10 = promoters)

- Faster archivals = better employer brand perception
- Companies that archive faster are likely more efficient across other hiring process points too — correlation likely has many related factors

### Time to Archive — Overall Benchmarks (Stable Over 5 Years)

- Non-interviewed candidates: median **~6 days**
- Interviewed candidates: median **~21 days**
- Flat trend over 5 years = opportunity for optimization, not a ceiling
- Non-interviewed candidates can be handled via automatic archival rules
- Interviewed candidates involve more people and decisions — different process and different benchmarks warranted

### Distribution

- ~**58% of non-interviewed candidates are archived within first 9 days**
- Numbers drop off quickly after that
- **Spike at 150+ days** — meaningful share of candidates sitting unarchived beyond any reasonable window; these candidates fell through the cracks
- This is a data quality concern and a candidate experience liability — every candidate in the long tail never got closure

### Time to Archive by Source

- **Inbound and referral:** expected pattern — no interview = archived quickly; interviewed = takes longer
- **Sourced candidates:** outlier — remarkably consistent at 14–17 days whether they had an interview or not
  - More likely a system behavior than a recruiter behavior
  - Outbound campaigns set to reach out at regular intervals; archival timing follows campaign cadence regardless of outcome
  - If sourced candidates are archiving on campaign cadence rather than intentional rules, workflow automation can configure source-specific logic

## Conclusion

Through analyzing 54M+ applications and 93K jobs (Jan 2021–Mar 2026):

- Hiring predictability improves as orgs scale — TTF stabilizes as systems mature
- Time to Hire is shaped by accumulated small delays, not one bottleneck
- Interview design and job function play a significant role in efficiency — scheduling method and panel complexity materially impact timelines
- Referrals consistently outperform other sources on passthrough AND offer acceptance
- For RecOps: the goal is designing and maintaining a cohesive system that balances efficiency, signal quality, and candidate experience — not just optimizing individual steps
